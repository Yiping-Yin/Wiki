# Loom Negative-Space Wordmark · Brand Design

Status: proposed, supersedes the earlier connective-stroke exploration  
Updated: 2026-04-17

## 1. Decision

Loom's next wordmark direction should move away from:

- clever local ligatures
- visible "mechanism" drawn in positive strokes
- explanatory woven-line graphics
- AI-generated premium-looking wordmarks that still behave like ordinary fonts

The new direction is a **negative-space absorption wordmark**.

The governing idea is not:

- "letters connected in an interesting way"

It is:

- a stable system field
- an external fragment that has not yet fully settled
- that fragment becoming absorbed and rewritten
- a quiet final state after absorption

The wordmark should therefore be designed less by **adding gestures** and more by **withholding and reshaping form**.

## 2. Why The Previous Route Failed

The previous route had concept, but not enough design.

Its recurring failure mode was:

- the cleverness lived in one local connection
- the rest of the word still behaved like a stylish custom font
- the result looked "designed" but did not feel inevitable

That is not enough.

Loom needs a mark whose intelligence is present in the **state change across the whole word**, not in a single stroke flourish.

## 3. Core Brand Claim

Loom is valuable because it takes jumpy, discontinuous thought and lets it sediment into an internal system of memory and judgment.

The correct visual metaphor is therefore not speed, trajectory, or visible weaving action.

It is **absorption**:

- a field exists
- something partially outside that field is pulled into order
- the result is quieter and more stable than the incoming fragment

This should feel inward, not performative.

## 4. Chosen Formal Direction

### 4.1 Mark Type

Use a **lowercase custom wordmark** based on `loom`.

The wordmark must be:

- structural
- symbolic
- restrained

It must not behave like a normal font sample.

### 4.2 Primary Formal Trick

The central intelligence of the mark should live in **negative space and state difference**, not in an obvious positive-stroke trick.

The mark should feel as though:

- the first `o` is not fully resolved
- the second `o` has been rewritten into a more stable state
- the transition between them is not a normal connection but a controlled reconfiguration

The "aha" should come from realizing that the two `o` forms are not copies. They represent two different states of the same idea.

### 4.3 Reading Order

The reading order should be:

1. first read: a calm, high-end custom wordmark
2. second read: something is structurally different between the two `o` forms
3. third read: the word encodes absorption, rewriting, and settlement

If the mechanism is obvious at first glance, it is too explanatory.

## 5. Letter-Level Rules

### 5.1 `l`

- `l` is the system field or axis
- it must be the calmest, most stable form in the word
- it should not be dramatic
- it should not look handwritten, flourish-led, or performative

### 5.2 first `o`

- should feel slightly external to the final system
- may be less closed, less stable, or less optically complete
- must not read as a damaged circle
- should feel intentionally unresolved

### 5.3 second `o`

- should be visibly related to the first `o`, but not identical
- should feel more absorbed, more rewritten, and more settled
- must not be a duplication of the first shape
- should make the viewer feel that the system has acted on it

### 5.4 `m`

- must be low, wide, and quiet
- should feel like the sedimented result, not a decorative finish
- should not peak, jump, or call attention to itself
- should feel like ground after transformation

## 6. Formal Constraints

The wordmark should pursue:

- black and white studies first
- thin to medium precision
- controlled rhythm
- generous white space
- slight asymmetry
- subtle editorial intelligence without becoming italic

Avoid:

- glow
- texture-led sophistication
- over-rendered presentation
- ordinary geometric-sans behavior
- perfect circles
- perfect symmetry
- obvious ligatures
- calligraphy
- flourish
- cat / face reads
- pointed `m` peaks
- speed or trajectory cues
- visible weave diagrams

This direction should be proved first in pure form, not mood.

## 7. What "Advanced" Means Here

For this direction, "advanced" means:

- less shown
- more implied
- stronger proportion decisions
- clearer state difference with fewer moves

The mark should feel less like a logo trying to explain itself and more like a brand asset that has already decided what matters.

## 8. Evaluation Criteria

The next rounds should be judged against these tests:

1. Does the mark still look like a real piece of identity design, not a concept diagram?
2. Do the two `o` forms feel like different states, not different decorations?
3. Is the mechanism carried by omission and reshaping, not by obvious joining?
4. Does the mark feel calm and premium before the concept is understood?
5. Is `m` genuinely sedimented and quiet?
6. Would the mark still be interesting in flat black on white with no effects?

If any answer is no, the design is not ready.

## 9. Next Iteration Brief

The next exploration should:

- begin as black-on-white structural studies
- avoid all glow, texture, and premium-presentation effects
- treat `l` as stable field
- make the first `o` intentionally less resolved
- make the second `o` the absorbed and rewritten form
- keep `m` low, wide, and settled
- search for intelligence in negative space, omission, and proportion
- optimize for "design feeling" before storytelling

The next round should aim for:

- less ligature
- less explanation
- more proportion
- more state difference
- more inevitability
