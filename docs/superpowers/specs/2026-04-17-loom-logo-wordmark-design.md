# Loom Logo Wordmark · Brand Design

Status: proposed, revised after concept-direction decision  
Updated: 2026-04-17

## 1. Decision

Loom's primary mark should move away from:

- a generic monoline `Loom` wordmark
- a cat-like monogram
- a decorative weave illustration
- a separate icon-plus-type treatment where the concept lives only in the icon

The chosen direction is a **structural lowercase wordmark** where the concept lives inside the lettering itself.

The governing concept is:

- **通经断纬**
- human thought is jumpy and relational
- memory becomes valuable when those jumps are absorbed into a lasting internal system

So the mark should express:

- a **continuous organizing structure**
- **local discontinuous thought fragments**
- those fragments being **taken in and sedimented into one system**

## 2. Core Brand Claim

Loom is not merely "weaving" in the abstract.

Loom is the act of taking discontinuous thought and letting it become part of a continuous system of memory and judgment.

The mark should therefore feel like:

- a stable structure already exists
- local fragments appear, attach, and are organized
- the final form is coherent because the system holds it together

This is closer to a structural metaphor than an illustrative metaphor.

## 3. Ingenuity Target

The desired level of cleverness is closer to the logic behind the `SpaceX` wordmark than to a normal custom logotype.

That means the mark should contain **one central formal trick**:

- a single critical stroke should carry **two meanings at once**

Surface meaning:

- it is the structural backbone of the wordmark

Deeper meaning:

- it performs the action of absorbing discontinuous fragments back into a stable system

The mark should not need an extra symbol, comet, sparkle, or annotation to explain that idea.

## 4. Chosen Formal Direction

### 4.1 Mark Type

Use a **lowercase structural wordmark** based on `loom`.

Do not build a separate emblem first and then place a generic word beside it.

This decision explicitly beats the explored alternatives:

- Italian italic as primary direction
- flourish serif as primary direction
- English calligraphic lettering as primary direction

Those references may inform tension or rhythm, but they should not become the dominant typographic genre of the mark.

### 4.2 Primary Visual Logic

The whole word should feel as though it has been generated by one governing structural line.

That line should:

1. establish order
2. deviate with purpose
3. absorb a fragment
4. return to order

This is the logo's core mechanism.

### 4.3 Reading Order

The mark should reveal itself in layers:

1. first read: a calm, stable brand structure
2. second read: a system taking in jumpy fragments
3. third read: the word `loom`

If the word reads first and the structure reads second, the design is too ordinary.

### 4.4 Typographic Attitude

The wordmark should remain a **structural custom logotype**, not a font-forward styling exercise.

The chosen attitude is:

- structural wordmark first
- slight editorial intelligence second
- no overt flourish
- no literal handwriting

The intended motion is a **light forward lean**, not a true italic construction.

That means:

- the mark may carry a small amount of forward tension
- the main axis must still feel stable
- the effect should suggest thought progressing into order
- it must not read like a magazine masthead, heritage calligraphy, or fashion logotype

## 5. Letter-Level Rules

### 5.1 `l`

- `l` is the main axis
- it is the tallest and calmest part of the mark
- it represents the persistent system or warp structure
- it should not look decorative or calligraphic

### 5.2 first `o`

- not a perfect neutral circle
- should feel like a local loop or fragment that has been pulled into alignment
- may be partial or tensioned by the governing line

### 5.3 second `o`

- must not mirror the first `o`
- should feel related, not duplicated
- should express linkage and continuation rather than symmetry

### 5.4 `m`

- low, wide, and settled
- should feel like the result of the governing line returning to structure
- must not produce ear-like peaks or a decorative flourish

## 6. Form Constraints

The visual language remains:

- monoline
- rounded terminals
- warm ivory line on a deep navy/near-black field
- quiet, premium, system-brand tone
- slight forward tension may be present, but only in service of the structure

Avoid:

- eyes, dots, whiskers, face-like symmetry
- cat or animal associations
- pointed `M` peaks
- perfect bilateral symmetry
- overtly illustrative weave diagrams
- comet imagery inside the static mark
- literal italic genre behavior
- flourish-led serif behavior
- calligraphic swash logic
- too many crossings
- ornamental cleverness without structural payoff

Allow only the minimum number of crossings or attachments needed to express absorption into system.

## 7. What "Clever" Means Here

For Loom, "clever" does **not** mean:

- hiding a random icon in negative space
- adding a secondary symbol
- making the word hard to read
- introducing decorative futurism

For Loom, "clever" means:

- the same structural move solves typography and meaning
- the brand idea is embedded in the construction of the word
- the mark feels inevitable after explanation

The user response should be:

- "I see it now"

not:

- "I understand the story because it was described to me"

## 8. System Usage

### 8.1 Static Mark

The static primary mark should carry:

- structure
- memory sedimentation
- continuous warp / broken weft logic

It should not carry comet motion.

### 8.2 Motion / Supporting Visual Language

Comet logic belongs in:

- launch animation
- insight or crystallization moments
- ambient campaign visuals

It should remain a supporting motion-language element, not the core skeleton of the static logo.

### 8.3 App Icon

The app icon may eventually extract a tighter emblematic form from the structural wordmark, but the concept should be validated in the wordmark first.

## 9. Evaluation Criteria

The next rounds should be judged against these tests:

1. Does the mark avoid accidental animal or face associations?
2. Does one structural stroke clearly dominate the wordmark?
3. Does the word feel system-generated rather than letter-by-letter assembled?
4. Is there an actual "aha" mechanism, not just tasteful refinement?
5. Does the mark remain calm, premium, and legible at app scale?
6. Would the concept survive if the viewer never heard the explanation "通经断纬"?

If any answer is no, the design is not ready.

## 10. Next Iteration Brief

The next generated exploration should:

- redesign the full lowercase `loom` as a structural wordmark
- let one governing stroke dominate the construction
- make `l` the stable axis
- make the two `o` forms asymmetrical, relational, and visibly organized by that stroke
- make `m` a low, wide, settled return to order
- reduce ordinary wordmark behavior
- increase the sense that the word has been structurally written by one system
- introduce only a slight forward tension, without becoming a true italic or calligraphic mark
- keep the mark minimal, premium, and vector-clean

The next iteration should aim for **less explanation, more mechanism**.
