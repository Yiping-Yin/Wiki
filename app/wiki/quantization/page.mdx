# 12 · Quantization — Squeezing LLMs Onto Your Laptop

Quantization replaces fp16/bf16 weights with low-bit integers (int8, int4, sometimes int2), trading a tiny bit of quality for **4×–8× memory savings** and often faster inference. It's what makes a 70B model fit on a single consumer GPU.

## The basic scheme: linear / affine quantization

For a tensor $x$ with range $[\alpha, \beta]$, pick a scale $s$ and zero point $z$:

$$
s = \frac{\beta - \alpha}{2^b - 1}, \qquad z = \text{round}\!\left(-\frac{\alpha}{s}\right)
$$

Then quantize and dequantize:

$$
q = \text{round}\!\left(\frac{x}{s}\right) + z, \qquad \hat{x} = s\,(q - z)
$$

## Why per-channel / per-group matters

A single scale for an entire weight matrix is a disaster — outlier columns blow up the quantization range. Solutions in increasing sophistication:

1. **Per-tensor** (don't do this for LLMs)
2. **Per-channel** (per output column) — standard for int8
3. **Per-group** (groups of 32–128 elements) — used by GPTQ, AWQ, and bitsandbytes int4

## The four real techniques

| Method | Bits | Calibration | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **bitsandbytes 8-bit** | 8 | none | drop-in, ~free |
| **GPTQ** | 4 | one-shot Hessian-based | lossy ~1% PPL |
| **AWQ** | 4 | activation-aware | preserves salient channels |
| **QLoRA** | 4 (NF4) | none + dequant in fwd | enables fine-tuning of int4 weights |

<Callout type="info">
Outlier features in transformer activations mean naive int8 fails on models &gt; ~6.7B. LLM.int8() handles this by keeping outlier columns in fp16 — that one trick is what unlocked the entire 4-bit ecosystem.
</Callout>
